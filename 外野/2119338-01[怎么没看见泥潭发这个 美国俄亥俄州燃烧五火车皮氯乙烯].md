436 ft (133 m)<strong>Times Beach</strong> is a [ghost town](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghost_town) in [St. Louis County](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Louis_County,_Missouri), [Missouri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Missouri), United States, 17 miles (27 km) southwest of [St. Louis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri) and 2 miles (3 km) east of [Eureka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eureka,_Missouri). Once home to more than two thousand people, the town was completely evacuated early in 1983 due to [TCDD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2,3,7,8-Tetrachlorodibenzodioxin)—also known as [dioxin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dioxins_and_dioxin-like_compounds)—contamination. It was the largest civilian exposure to the compound in the history of the United States.In 1985, the State of Missouri officially disincorporated the city of Times Beach. The site of Times Beach now houses a 419-acre (170 ha) [state park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Route_66_State_Park) commemorating [U.S. Route 66](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Route_66)—the famous highway that stretched from [Chicago, Illinois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago,_Illinois), to [Santa Monica, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Santa_Monica,_California), and passed by the community on its southern end—as well as the history of the Times Beach area.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Remember-1) The park opened in 1999.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Encyc-2) In 2001, the EPA removed Times Beach from its [Superfund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfund) list.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-3)<strong>History[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Times_Beach,_Missouri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1)]</strong>Times Beach was founded in 1925 on the [flood plain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flood_plain) of the [Meramec River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meramec_River), southwest of the river, in a promotion by the now-defunct [St. Louis Star-Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Louis_Star-Times) newspaper. A purchase of a 20 × 100 ft (6 by 30 m) lot for $67.50 included a six-month newspaper subscription.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Remember-1)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-SR-4)In its early years, the town was primarily a summer [resort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resort_town), but the [Great Depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Depression) combined with gasoline [rationing during World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationing_in_the_United_States) reduced the feasibility of [summer homes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summer_home).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-SR-4) The town became a community of mostly [low-income housing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-income_housing), and a small population (1,240) lived in Times Beach by 1970.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-SR-4) In the years immediately before its evacuation, Times Beach had become a lower-middle-class town.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-SR-4) Historically, there had always been a small grocery store and gas station on Route 66 to serve the residents.[[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed)]Prone to flooding throughout its history—its first buildings were built on stilts[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-SR-4)—the town experienced a devastating flood in December 1982. It happened just as the [Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency) was confirming that dioxin contaminated the soil,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-5) leading to the town's evacuation by 1985[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Encyc-2) and complete demolition by 1992.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-6) The town was disincorporated by executive order of [Missouri governor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Missouri) [John Ashcroft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Ashcroft) on April 2, 1985.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-7)The story of Times Beach was featured on [History Channel's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_(U.S._TV_channel)) [Modern Marvels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_Marvels), in the episode "Engineering Disasters 20".[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-8)<strong>Dioxin contamination[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Times_Beach,_Missouri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2)]</strong><strong>Events leading up to the crisis[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Times_Beach,_Missouri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3)]</strong><strong>NEPACCO chemical waste disposal[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Times_Beach,_Missouri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4)]</strong>During the late 1960s, the [Northeastern Pharmaceutical and Chemical Company, Inc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NEPACCO). (NEPACCO) began operating out of a facility located near [Verona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verona,_Missouri), in southwestern Missouri. This facility was owned by Hoffman-Taff, a company that produced the [Agent Orange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agent_Orange) herbicide for use during the [Vietnam War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vietnam_War).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9) By the time NEPACCO ceased its operations in 1972, Hoffman-Taff had been taken over by Syntex Agribusiness.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hites-10)From 1970 to 1972, NEPACCO was primarily involved in the production of [hexachlorophene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexachlorophene) <strong>(3)</strong>, an antibacterial agent used in soap, toothpaste, and common household disinfectants, from 2,4,5-trichlorophenol <strong>(1)</strong> and formaldehyde <strong>(2)</strong>.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hites-10)<[img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Hexachlorophenesynthesis.png/400px-Hexachlorophenesynthesis.png" referrerpolicy="no-referrer">

2,4,5-trichlorophenol is synthesized from 1,2,4,5-tetrachlorobenzene by the [nucleophilic aromatic substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleophilic_aromatic_substitution) reaction with sodium hydroxide (NaOH). Unfortunately, instantaneous [dimerization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry)) of the resulting [phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol) produces trace amounts of [2,3,7,8-tetrachlorodibenzo-p-dioxin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2,3,7,8-tetrachlorodibenzo-p-dioxin) (TCDD), an extremely toxic compound known to have both acute and chronic adverse effects.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hites-10)<[img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Seveso_reaction_de.svg/400px-Seveso_reaction_de.svg.png" referrerpolicy="no-referrer">

Beginning the process of production with 2,4,5-trichlorophenol that contained 3–5 parts per million (ppm) of dioxin, NEPACCO was able to reduce the concentration of dioxin in hexachlorophene to 0.1 ppm.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11) The result of this purification process led to the storage and accumulation of heavily concentrated dioxin still bottoms, or thick, oily residues, in a storage tank located near the facility in Verona. When NEPACCO first began operations, the still bottoms were sent to a waste facility in Louisiana for incineration.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9) Although incineration was the best method to destroy dioxins at the time, it was also very expensive. Looking for less costly alternatives, NEPACCO contracted the services of the Independent Petrochemical Corporation (IPC).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11) However, IPC, a chemical supplier company, knew very little about waste disposal, and subcontracted the NEPACCO job to Russell Martin Bliss, the owner of a small, local waste oil business. Charging NEPACCO $3000 per load, IPC paid Bliss $125 per load.Between February and October 1971, Bliss collected six truckloads (nearly 18,500 gallons) of chemical waste heavily contaminated with dioxin. Bliss took most of the still bottoms to his storage facility near [Frontenac, Missouri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frontenac,_Missouri), where the contaminated NEPACCO waste was unloaded and mixed into tanks containing used motor oils. Subsequently, some of the contaminated oil was sold to MT Richards, a fuel company in Illinois, and to the Midwest Oil Refining Company in [Overland, Missouri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overland,_Missouri).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9)<strong>First cases of contamination[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Times_Beach,_Missouri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5)]</strong>In addition to his waste oil business, Bliss owned a horse arena and farm, where he sprayed waste oils to control the problem of dust. One application kept the dust down for several months. Those who visited Bliss' property were impressed by how well the technique worked. It was not long before people began to hire him for his dust-suppressant services.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11)On May 26, 1971, the owners of Shenandoah Stable, located near [Moscow Mills, Missouri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow_Mills,_Missouri), Judy Piatt and Frank Hampel, paid Bliss $150 to spray the floor of their indoor arena.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Sun-12) The waste oil sprayed, which totaled a volume of 2,000 gallons, was uncharacteristically thick, and left a pungent, burning odor.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Allen-13) Within a few days of the spraying, birds began to drop dead from the rafters of the barns, and horses began to develop sores and lose their hair. Piatt and Hampel blamed these occurrences on Bliss, who denied responsibility, claiming that the material he sprayed was nothing more than old motor oil.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9)Acting on their suspicions, Piatt and Hampel removed the top six inches of soil from the entire arena and disposed of it in a landfill. Despite the removal of another 12 inches of soil a few months later, the horses that came to the arena still became ill.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11) After several months, 62 horses died or became so emaciated that they had to be euthanized. Hampel, Piatt, and Piatt's two young daughters also became ill, developing headaches, nosebleeds, abdominal pain, and diarrhea.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Allen-13)A month after the spraying at Shenandoah, Bliss was hired to spray the arena at Timberline Stables, near [Jefferson City, Missouri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jefferson_City,_Missouri). Twelve horses died, and children exposed to the arena were diagnosed with [chloracne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloracne), a skin condition associated with [dioxin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dioxin) poisoning.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Suskind-14) Suspecting that Bliss' oil was the source of their problems, the owners of Timberline removed the top layer of soil from their property.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11)A third arena, at Bubbling Springs Ranch, near St. Louis, was also sprayed around the same time as Timberline and faced similar problems. As at Shenandoah and Timberline, the owners decided to remove the top layer of soil from their arena. Vernon Stout, a road-grading contractor, completed the removal in March 1973. Instead of bringing the soil to a landfill, however, Stout unloaded the soil onto one of his properties and at the nearby home of Harold Minker.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11)<strong>CDC investigations[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Times_Beach,_Missouri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6)]</strong>The unexplained deaths and illnesses at Shenandoah immediately caught the attention of the [Centers for Disease Control and Prevention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention) (CDC). In August 1971, the CDC completed an inspection of Shenandoah Stable, and collected human and animal blood samples, as well as soil samples.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9) Although initial results revealed that the soil at Shenandoah Stable contained PCBs and chlorinated insecticides, the CDC was unable to identify a specific chemical culprit. It was not until 1973 that tests revealed the presence of [trichlorophenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichlorophenol).[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Allen-13) When trace amounts of the crude trichlorophenol contaminant were administered to the inner surfaces of rabbit ears, blisters developed, which was a characteristic result of trichlorophenol poisoning.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9) The unexpected death of some of the affected rabbits however, led the CDC to run more complex tests.On July 30, 1974, the CDC found that, in addition to 5,000 ppm of trichlorophenol and 1,590 ppm of [PCBs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl), the soil samples collected from Shenandoah contained over 30 ppm of dioxin.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Allen-13) Although little was known about the effects of dioxin on humans, the lethality of small doses in animals was alarming. As a result, the CDC immediately set out to locate other possible sites of contamination. When confronted by the CDC, Bliss stated that he did not know where the dioxin could have come from.Because dioxin was a by-product of only a handful of chemicals, trichlorophenol being the most common, the CDC narrowed their search to companies in Missouri using or producing trichlorophenol. NEPACCO was the only company on the search list that had come into contact with Bliss.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11)NEPACCO went out of business in 1972, after the [Food and Drug Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration) (FDA) had issued a ban that limited the use of hexachlorophene. The ban was motivated by the death of thirty-two infants in France who were exposed to high levels of hexachlorophene in baby powder.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9) During an inspection of the old NEPACCO facility in Verona, which was now entirely owned by Syntex Agribusiness, the CDC discovered an old tank filled with 4,300 gallons of NEPACCO still bottoms at a dioxin concentration of more than 340 ppm. Due to a lack of incinerators in proximity to the Verona plant, the proper disposal of the leftover still bottoms would not be completed until 1979.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11)With further investigation of contaminated locations, the CDC advised residents of the Minker and Stout properties to minimize contact with the soil. Soil samples had revealed a dioxin concentration of 0.85 ppm at Minker's property, and a concentration of 0.44 ppm at Stout's property. In a 1975 confidential report to the [Environmental Protection Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency) (EPA), the CDC also further advised the removal and burial of contaminated soil from both properties. In the same document, however, the CDC reported that the [half-life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-life) of dioxin was one year.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Wildavsky-15) Based on this estimate, which was later found to be incorrect, Missouri officials decided to forgo the recommended cleanup.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11) Today, the half-life of dioxin is estimated to be seven to eleven years.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-16)<strong>Involvement of the EPA[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Times_Beach,_Missouri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7)]</strong>The EPA did not become heavily involved with the Missouri dioxin contaminations until 1979 when a former NEPACCO employee reported the burial of toxic waste on a farm located about seven miles from Verona. NEPACCO had paid James Denney, the owner of the farm, $150 for the use of his property. Under the investigations of the EPA, a total of 90 drums, all of which were corroded and leaky, were unearthed. Eleven of these drums contained still bottoms with dioxin concentrations as high as 2,000 ppm.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Wildavsky-15)In May and June 1982, the EPA decided to revisit the Shenandoah, Timberline, and Bubbling Springs stables, as well as the Minker and Stout properties. New soil samples revealed that concentrations of dioxin had not decreased since the last test results obtained by the CDC several years earlier. Although eleven years had passed since the spraying, areas in the Shenandoah arena still contained as much as 1.8 ppm of dioxin. Regional EPA officials advised owners of the contaminated stables to temporarily close and urged the national EPA office to begin cleanup operations at each of the contaminated locations. These cleanup requests were delayed when [Rita Lavelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rita_Lavelle), an assistant administrator at EPA headquarters in Washington, announced that the EPA would be collecting and testing an additional 600 soil samples in order to better understand the extent of contamination.[[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed)]The EPA's handling of the events in Missouri caught national attention in late 1982 when the [Environmental Defense Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_Defense_Fund), a public interest group, published a leaked EPA document that listed a total of 14 confirmed and 41 possibly contaminated sites in the state of Missouri. The town of Times Beach was one of the locations listed. The Environmental Defense Fund also claimed that the EPA had decided to lower dioxin cleanup protocol standards. Although the CDC advised for cleanups in locations where dioxin levels were as low as 0.001 ppm, the Environmental Defense Fund report suggested that new EPA protocols would only necessitate clean up in locations where dioxin concentrations were above 0.1 ppm.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9)<strong>Crisis at Times Beach[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Times_Beach,_Missouri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8)]</strong><strong>Double disaster[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Times_Beach,_Missouri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9)]</strong>In 1972, Times Beach hired Bliss to oil its 23 miles of dirt roads (due to lack of funding, Times Beach was unable to pave its roads). For $2,400, Bliss sprayed approximately 160,000 gallons of waste oil in Times Beach over a period of four years.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Wildavsky-15) The release of the leaked EPA document in 1982 was the first time that Times Beach had learned of its contamination.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Allen-13) Residents felt betrayed and publicly criticized the EPA for not informing them of the toxic hazards around their homes.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Wildavsky-15) Since Times Beach had the largest population out of the listed sites, Times Beach became the subject of national media and attention.[[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed)]With pressure from the public, the EPA soon began investigation in Times Beach. Soil sampling was fortuitously completed on December 3, 1982, a day before Times Beach suffered its worst flood in history when the [Meramec River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meramec_River) breached its banks and rose over 14 feet above [flood stage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flood_stage).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Sun-12)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Wildavsky-15) The residents of Times Beach were evacuated, and by the time the waters began to recede, the EPA had concluded its analysis. Results revealed dioxin concentrations as high as 0.3 ppm along the town's entire network of roads.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11)<strong>Buyout and cleanup efforts[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Times_Beach,_Missouri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10)]</strong>On December 23, 1982, the CDC publicly recommended that Times Beach not be re-inhabited. Officials were uncertain about the health effects of extensive dioxin exposure and even more uncertain of how to rid an entire town of dioxin. Because the town was situated on a flood plain, officials were further concerned that subsequent flooding would spread the contamination beyond control.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9)Discussions of a federal buyout commenced on January 7, 1983, when President [Ronald Reagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Reagan) created the Times Beach Dioxin Task Force, which consisted of representatives from the EPA, CDC, [Federal Emergency Management Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Emergency_Management_Agency) (FEMA), and [Army Corps of Engineers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers). During a press conference on February 22, 1983, the EPA announced that the federal government would pay $33.0 million of the estimated $36.7 million cost to buy out the 800 residential properties and 30 businesses of Times Beach. The remaining $3.7 million would be the responsibility of the state.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Wildavsky-15)By 1985, Times Beach's entire population of well over 2,000 residents had been relocated, and [Governor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Missouri) [John Ashcroft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Ashcroft) had issued an executive order for the town's dis-incorporation.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-17)Since it contained over 50 percent of the dioxin in the state of Missouri and because it was no longer inhabited, Times Beach was the logical choice for the placement of a new incinerator. The construction of the incinerator began in June 1995. Once built, it burned more than 265,000 tons of dioxin-contaminated materials from across the state. The cleanup of Missouri was completed in 1997 and had cost close to $200 million.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Allen-13)<strong>Legal action and responsibility[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Times_Beach,_Missouri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11)]</strong>In response to the events that transpired in Missouri during the 1970s, a number of laws were passed to regulate the generation and disposal of potentially hazardous products. In 1976, Congress passed the [Toxic Substances Control Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxic_Substances_Control_Act), which required the testing of chemicals that could pose an unreasonable risk to the environment. In 1976, Congress also passed the [Resource Conservation and Recovery Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resource_Conservation_and_Recovery_Act) (RCRA), which regulated the transportation and disposal of hazardous waste.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Wildavsky-15) In 1980, the passage of the [Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comprehensive_Environmental_Response,_Compensation,_and_Liability_Act) (CERCLA) established a multibillion-dollar [Superfund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfund) to investigate and clean up old, abandoned hazardous waste sites.The passage of CERCLA also defined the liability of a company and ensured that parties responsible for the release of toxic substances are held liable in the case of environmental damage or harm. In 1983, the federal government sued NEPACCO and its officers, Edwin Michaels and John W. Lee, in United States v. Northeastern Pharmaceutical and Chemical Co.. Under the provisions of CERCLA, NEPACCO was forced to repay the federal government for its cleanup efforts at the farm of James Denney, the site where NEPACCO had buried ninety drums of its chemical waste over a decade earlier.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-20)Because the RCRA was not implemented until 1976, Bliss was not legally required to keep records of the chemical wastes he had collected from NEPACCO. During investigations surrounding the dioxin contamination in Missouri, Bliss maintained that he had no knowledge of the presence of dioxin in the chemical waste he collected from NEPACCO.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Sun-12) Still, Bliss was the object of many legal pursuits. Over 14,000 citizens' suits were filed against NEPACCO and its officers, Syntex Agribusiness, IPC, and Bliss.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Gough-11) Among these were the cases of Piatt and Hample, who in 1976 settled their suit against Bliss for $10,000 and against IPC for $100,000. In 1981, Piatt and Hample also settled for $65,000 from NEPACCO. IPC paid $1 million to each of Piatt's daughters in 1983.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Wildavsky-15)<strong>Consequences of the contamination[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Times_Beach,_Missouri&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12)]</strong>Although the decision for relocation in 1982 was made in the best interests and safety of the Times Beach residents, the evacuation was not an easy transition. Eight hundred families had to leave their lives completely behind. Initially, parents worried about what to do and where to go for financial assistance. As they began to settle into their new lives, their logistical and financial worries were soon replaced by the fear that their children would be afflicted by sudden chronic illnesses. The psychological trauma caused by relocation was immeasurable.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Times_Beach,_Missouri#cite_note-Hernan-9)<[img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Route_66_Times_Beach_visitor_center.jpg/220px-Route_66_Times_Beach_visitor_center.jpg" referrerpolicy="no-referrer">[size=12.3704px]
